Problem Statement

Product teams often rely on dashboards filled with multiple metrics ‚Äî daily active users, feature usage, retention curves, and engagement rates ‚Äî yet still struggle to make clear product decisions.

While data is available, the absence of a clear North Star metric and structured growth framework leads to scattered experimentation, conflicting priorities, and short-term optimizations that fail to improve long-term retention.

This creates a core challenge: How can product teams use analytics not just to measure performance, but to guide meaningful growth decisions?

My Role

Independent Product Analytics (Case Study):

Led an independent product analytics and growth strategy case study, responsible for defining the problem, identifying key success metrics, analyzing user behavior patterns, and translating analytical insights into actionable growth and product strategy recommendations.

Approach

  • Defined the core growth problem and success criteria
  • Analyzed the user journey across activation, engagement, and retention stages
  • Identified key drop-off points using funnel and cohort thinking
  • Evaluated metrics to distinguish vanity metrics from actionable signals
  • Defined a North Star metric aligned with long-term value
  • Proposed data-backed growth strategies and experiments

Key Insights

  • Not all metrics are decision-making metrics.
    Teams often track what is easy to measure rather than what reflects real user value.
  • Retention is a stronger growth signal than short-term activity.
    High daily activity does not guarantee long-term engagement or habit formation.
  • Activation quality matters more than activation volume.
    Users who reach meaningful first success early are significantly more likely to retain.
  • Feature usage metrics can be misleading in isolation.
    A frequently used feature does not always correlate with long-term product stickiness.
  • North Star metrics create alignment across teams.
    A single guiding metric helps product, design, and engineering prioritize consistently.

Product Decisions & Trade-offs

  • Chose retention-focused metrics over raw engagement metrics.
    Prioritized signals tied to long-term value instead of surface-level activity.
  • Chose a single North Star metric instead of multiple success metrics.
    Accepted reduced visibility in exchange for a stronger strategic focus.
  • Chose cohort analysis over snapshot reporting.
    Traded real-time simplicity for deeper behavioural understanding.
  • Chose behavioral milestones over feature-level KPIs.
    Focused on outcomes users achieve rather than features they click.

Outcome / Impact

Based on the analytics framework and insights, the proposed strategy was expected to:

  • Improve long-term retention by focusing teams on meaningful user success events
  • Reduce metric overload and decision confusion across product discussions
  • Enable clearer prioritization of growth experiments
  • Align cross-functional teams around a shared definition of product success

Note: These outcomes represent projected impact based on analytical reasoning and assumptions, not actual production data.

What I Learned

  • Data alone does not drive growth ‚Äî interpretation does.
    Metrics only create value when connected to decisions.
  • North Star metrics simplify complexity.
    One clear metric often outperforms dozens of dashboards.
  • Growth is a product problem, not a marketing problem.
    Sustainable growth emerges from user value, not acquisition alone.
  • Strong PM analytics focuses on behaviour, not numbers.
    Understanding why users act matters more than how many did.
  • Analytics should guide trade-offs, not just reporting.
    The real role of product analytics is prioritization, not observation.
